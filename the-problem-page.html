Incorrect E-Disposal Methods and Solutions

Coded and presented by Mayur Joshi of 8B

IB MYP Community Project

In a world filled with technology, most of it is thrown out; this negatively was, is, and will continue to affect ecosystems and human health worldwide. My project aims to increase awareness about overlooked issues like these to create a healthier world.

Phones, computers, and TVs contain toxic substances such as lead and mercury. While these things might seem like another type of trash, e-waste is far more dangerous than it sounds. Improper disposal, especially in countries without strong environmental laws, can result in serious health risks for workers and nearby communities. Improper recycling, where electronics are burned or taken apart without safety gear, releases harmful chemicals into the air, soil, and water. This contamination promotes environmental injustice, since these acts are usually located in low-income areas with little political power to fight back. Although there are global efforts like take-back programs and laws promoting Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR), a shocking 83% of global e-waste is never properly recycled. And, while the concept of "urban mining," which is to recover valuable metals like gold and copper from old electronics shows promise, it's still underused. One major challenge? Many people simply don't know where or how to recycle their electronics. Convenience, awareness, and access are all part of the problem. Meanwhile, richer countries usually ship their e-waste overseas, leaving poorer ones to deal with the toxicity problems. Taking over e-waste needs global cooperation, smarter design, and better education, and that starts with knowing the facts.
